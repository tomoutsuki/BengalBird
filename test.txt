IPA / ɔ /. As o in British English hOt. The letter is called স্বর-অ svoro-o in Bengali.

In Bengali orthography, in the absence of contra-indication and whether or not svoro-o is actually pronounced, every consonant sign by default comprises a consonant sound followed by an inherent svoro-o. Consequently, in Bengali orthography svoro-o is never actually written immediately after a consonant.

Where the difference in pronunciation between o in British English hOt and (see the next item) o in English Obey is to be shown, svoro-o with the pronunciation of IPA / ɔ / can be written as ó.

IPA / ~ /. Nasalisation of vowel or diphthong, as n in French viN / vɛ̃ / (“wine”). The letter is called চন্দ্রবিন্দু condrovindu (“moon [and] dot”) in Bengali. Condrovindu is written in the Romanisation next after the vowel or diphthong it nasalises.

IPA / ʃ /. As sh in English caSH - the usual pronunciation in modern Bengali.

In medieval precursors to Iberian Romance languages, the letter x generally had the pronunciation / ʃ /. The letter in Portuguese most commonly still has the pronunciation / ʃ / - e.g. in caixa / kɑjʃɑ / (“box”, “cashier”). Likewise in Catalan - e.g. in xarop / ʃɑrop / (“syrup”) - and in Galician (Gallego) - e.g. in viaxe / bjɑʃe / (“journey”).

The non-Indo-European Basque language employs the pronunciation / ʃ / for the letter x - e.g. in xuri / ʃuri / (“white”). The Semitic Maltese language also assigns the pronunciation / ʃ / to the letter x - e.g. in xemxata / ʃɛmʃɐtɐ / (“sunstroke”).

Silent. The letter in Bengali orthography is silent when it occurs immediately before ঋ rwi, between a vowel and a  jzo-phola (the  jzo-phola in this circumstance will be Romanised as jz - see rule 11), or before any (other) consonant. The pronunciation will be doubled of any such  jzo-phola or other consonant (ঋ rwi is not a consonant), except at the start of a word.

If the অন্তঃস্থ হ ontohwstho ho occurs between a vowel and a ঋ rwi, the pronunciation will change of such ঋ rwi to IPA / rri / (as re-ri in English caRE-RIdden).

ROMAN LETTER NAMES

If a standard Romanisation becomes widespread to render Bengali for speakers of other languages, including speakers of languages other than English, one issue that should be considered is how to describe Roman letters in Bengali.

The alphabet used by the Romans for their language Latin has come to be used by numerous languages in and outside Europe: however, it is only the English names for Roman letters that are regularly used by Bengali speakers. But when those English names are phonetically rendered they often have no obvious correspondence with the Roman letters being described. The Roman alphabet descends from the alphabet used by the Semitic Phoenicians, from which alphabet also descends the alphabet used by the Semitic Arabs, and the expanded version of the Arabic alphabet used in Persian. Looking at the letter names used in Arabic, Persian, Latin and Bengali, and with the idea that letter names should have an obvious phonetic correspondence with the letters being named, something like the listing below could be used in Bengali to describe Roman letters.
